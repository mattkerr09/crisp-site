Restoring old home videos on a Mac

Home video is the one category where upscaling genuinely earns its reputation. The footage is soft, noisy, interlaced and irreplaceable — and unlike a modern phone clip, there is real room for improvement. Here's what actually helps, and what no tool can do.

Get the transfer right first

Everything downstream is limited by the capture. If you're digitising tapes yourself, capture at the highest quality your device offers and save a lossless or lightly compressed master before you touch anything. Every restoration you attempt should start from that master, not from a compressed export you made along the way.

Keep the original file forever, even after you've made a beautiful restored version. Tools improve. In five years you'll want to run the tape again through something better, and you can only do that if you kept the raw capture.

Deinterlace before you upscale

Tape and old camcorder footage is usually interlaced: each frame is two half-images captured a fraction of a second apart. Upscale that without deinterlacing first and the AI faithfully enlarges the comb-shaped tearing on every moving edge, making it more obvious rather than less.

Deinterlace first, then upscale. Getting this order wrong is the single most common reason a restoration looks worse than the original.

What AI can and cannot recover

It genuinely helps with: soft focus from a cheap lens, tape noise and colour speckle, mild compression damage from an old digitisation, and the general lack of sharpness that makes VHS look like VHS.

It cannot recover: a face that was six pixels wide, text on a sign that was never legible, or detail lost to a badly degraded tape. When a model "restores" those it is inventing plausible detail — which on a stranger's footage is fine, and on your grandmother's face is not. If a result looks impressively sharp but subtly wrong, trust that instinct and dial the strength back.

Be conservative with faces

Faces are the whole reason you're doing this, and they're where aggressive processing does the most damage. Over-sharpened skin goes waxy and the person stops looking like themselves. Always compare a still of a face before and after at full zoom, and prefer the gentler result — you're preserving a memory, not producing a demo reel.

Your family footage should not be uploaded

Most online upscalers require handing your files to a server. For holiday footage of your children, a wedding, or someone who has died, that's a genuinely different proposition from uploading a product demo — and the terms of service on free tools are rarely reassuring about what happens to what you upload.

A workable process

  1. Capture at the best quality available and archive that master untouched.
  2. Deinterlace.
  3. Trim the dead ends — tape starts and stops are usually minutes of nothing.
  4. Denoise gently, then upscale. In that order; upscaling noise just makes bigger noise.
  5. Compare faces at 100% against the original before you commit.
  6. Export one archival-quality copy and one smaller copy for sharing with family.
