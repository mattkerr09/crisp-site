Crisp for real estate

A listing video is doing one job: making a property look like somewhere worth visiting. Most agent footage is shot on a phone, walking, in mixed indoor light — and that combination produces three specific problems that are all fixable in a few minutes.

Stabilisation matters more than resolution

Handheld walking footage is the signature of an amateur listing, and it's the first thing to fix. A stabilised 1080p walkthrough looks more professional than shaky 4K, every time. Shoot in 4K if your phone allows it, because the extra pixels give the stabiliser room to crop into without softening the delivered image — then deliver 1080p.

Fix the verticals

Interiors are unforgiving about tilt. Door frames, window edges and worktops are dead straight in reality, so a camera held one degree off reads instantly as sloppy even to someone who couldn't say why. Straighten before you do anything else; it's the cheapest credibility in property video.

Windows are the exposure problem

Point a phone at a room with a bright window and it either exposes for the window and turns the room into a cave, or exposes for the room and blows the window into white. Expose for the room — buyers want to see the room — then lift the shadows slightly in the edit rather than trying to recover a window that was clipped to pure white, because that detail isn't recorded at all.

Keep it short and consistent

Portals and social feeds both punish long walkthroughs. Sixty to ninety seconds covering the main rooms outperforms a four-minute tour of every cupboard. If you list regularly, use the same order and the same look on every property — a recognisable style across listings does more for an agent's brand than any single video.

Client footage shouldn't be uploaded

Property interiors are people's homes, often still occupied, with their belongings in shot. Running those files through a browser upscaler means uploading the inside of someone's house to a third party. Crisp runs on your Mac with no upload and no account, which is the appropriate default for footage you were trusted with.

A repeatable ten-minute edit

  1. Import the walkthrough clips and drop the unusable takes.
  2. Stabilise anything shot walking.
  3. Straighten tilted shots.
  4. Trim each room to its best five to ten seconds.
  5. Apply one consistent look across all clips.
  6. Lift shadows slightly on interiors.
  7. Export 1080p — and a vertical crop if you post to social.

That's a single pass in Crisp at $19 one-time, with no subscription attached to a job you might only do twice a month.

Vertical is not optional any more

Most property discovery now happens on a phone, and portals increasingly surface vertical clips. Shooting 4K horizontal gives you enough pixels to crop a clean 1080x1920 version without the result looking soft, so you can deliver both from one shoot rather than filming everything twice.

Crop deliberately rather than centring. A centre crop of a room shot from the doorway usually lands on a blank wall, when the thing you wanted was the window or the kitchen island. Move the crop to the subject of each shot before exporting.

Music and voiceover

Silent walkthroughs feel unfinished, and phone audio inside an empty room is mostly echo and footsteps. Muting the clip and laying simple music underneath is nearly always better than the original sound. If you narrate, record the voiceover separately in a quiet room rather than talking while you walk — the difference in clarity is dramatic and costs nothing.
