How to make a split screen video on a Mac

Two clips, one frame. It's the format behind reaction videos, before-and-after comparisons, and the stacked clips that fill Reels and TikTok. The mechanics are simple; the things that make it look right are less obvious.

Pick the layout for the platform, not the footage

Vertical stack — one clip above the other, in a 9:16 frame. This is the social format: talking head on top, gameplay or satisfying b-roll underneath. It only works in portrait, and it's the layout most people actually mean.

Side by side — two clips left and right, landscape. Right for reaction videos, interviews across two cameras, and any genuine comparison where the viewer needs to see both at once at equal size.

Picture in picture — one clip full frame with a small inset. Best when one source is clearly primary, like a screen recording with a webcam corner.

The framing problem nobody warns you about

Two clips of different shapes will not fill their halves neatly. A landscape clip dropped into the top half of a vertical frame either gets cropped hard or leaves black bars, and a portrait clip in a side-by-side layout does the same.

Cropping to fill looks better than bars, but it means losing the sides of the shot — so check that the subject is still in frame after the crop. A talking head framed slightly off-centre is the classic casualty: perfectly composed in the original, half out of frame in the split.

Decide which audio survives

Two clips means two audio tracks, and playing both at once is almost always wrong. Usually one clip carries the voice and the other is silent b-roll, so mute the second entirely. For a reaction video where both matter, lower the source clip well under the reaction so the speech stays intelligible — competing dialogue at equal volume is unwatchable.

Syncing without timecode

If the two clips are meant to line up — two cameras on the same moment — find a sharp sound or a distinct visual event in both and align to that. A clap works because it's a hard spike in the waveform and a visible motion in the picture. Trim both clips to start from that point, then treat them as one.

Keeping it watchable

Two moving images compete for attention, which is the point on social and a problem everywhere else. If both halves are busy, the viewer watches neither. Pair an active clip with a calm one, and keep split-screen sections short — a whole video in split screen is exhausting to watch.

Matching two clips that don't match

Footage from two sources rarely agrees on colour. A phone and a camera pointed at the same room will differ in white balance enough that the split looks like two unrelated videos glued together. Nudge the warmer clip cooler, or the cooler one warmer, until skin tones roughly agree — this matters far more than getting either clip objectively correct.

Frame rate mismatch is the other one. A 60fps clip beside a 30fps clip will have visibly different motion, and there's no fixing that in the edit; decide which is primary and accept the difference, or reshoot.
