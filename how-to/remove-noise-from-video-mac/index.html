Denoise vs a plain blur

A simple blur hides noise by smearing everything, softening the whole frame. Crisp uses AI restoration — it removes grain while rebuilding edges and texture, so the result looks clean and sharp rather than mushy. Dial the Denoise slider to trade a little detail for a lot of cleanup, or vice-versa.

Grain, blocking and banding are different problems

"Noisy" covers three faults that need different fixes. Sensor grain is the fine speckle you get from shooting in low light — that's what denoising is for. Blocking is the mushy squares that appear in heavily compressed footage, which is a bitrate problem, not a noise one. Banding is visible steps in a gradient like a sky, and denoise usually makes it worse.

Look at a still frame before you reach for a slider. Fine speckle that shifts every frame is grain. Chunky squares that hold still for a moment are compression. Smooth stripes across a sky are banding.

Don't over-denoise

Heavy denoising is the most common way to make footage look worse. Push it too far and skin turns waxy, hair becomes a solid mass, and fine texture in fabric or foliage smears into paint. It reads as cheap immediately.

Work up from a light setting rather than down from a strong one, and judge on a still with faces in it — faces are where over-processing shows first.
