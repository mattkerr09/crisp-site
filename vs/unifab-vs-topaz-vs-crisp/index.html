What Topaz is actually selling

Not resolution — temporal consistency. Its models look at several frames together rather than upscaling each one independently, which is the difference between a result that holds still and one that shimmers in flat areas. That is a genuinely hard problem, it is the axis most reviews never test because it is invisible in screenshots, and Topaz is the best-known name at it for a reason.

If you restore footage professionally and $299/year is a line item on an invoice, that is a defensible purchase. The complaint that followed October 2025 was not about quality — it was that people who had bought a perpetual licence were moved onto a recurring one.

What UniFab is selling

Breadth. Twenty-two modules including DVD and Blu-ray ripping, format conversion, deinterlacing and audio tools, for a one-time $319.99. If you are digitising a shelf of discs, nothing else here competes and the price is fair for what it is.

The catch is Windows. UniFab's centre of gravity is NVIDIA GPUs on Windows, and a Mac user is buying a port. That matters more than it sounds for GPU-bound work.

What Crisp is selling

Being cheap enough not to think about, native to the machine, and a timeline in the same app — so you cut first and upscale the finished sequence once at export, rather than enhancing footage you later discard. Its heaviest mode peaks around 11GB, which is why it runs on a 16GB MacBook Air rather than needing a workstation.

What it does not have: Topaz's multi-frame temporal models, UniFab's disc tools, and any kind of multicam. On a hard restoration where shimmer is the problem, Topaz will likely give you a better frame.

Picking, honestly

  • Topaz if restoration is your job, shimmer is your enemy, and the fee is a business expense.
  • UniFab if you rip discs, or you genuinely want the wide toolkit, or you are mainly on Windows.
  • Crisp if you are on a Mac, want to own it, and would rather cut and clean in one place. It is free with a watermark, so the sensible move is to run your hardest clip through it before spending anything anywhere.

And if your footage is already sharp and only needs to be a different size, all three are overkill — a plain ffmpeg Lanczos resize is free, instant, and invents nothing.

The comparison nobody publishes

Every review in this category compares upscalers with paused screenshots, which is the one method guaranteed to miss the thing that separates them. Frames are upscaled independently, so a model's guesses vary slightly between consecutive frames, and in flat regions — a wall, sky, a plain jacket — that variation reads as a fine crawling shimmer. A still frame cannot show it. It is also precisely the axis Topaz's temporal models exist to win.

So when you test these yourself, use a clip with motion and a flat background, watch it at full size while it plays, and ignore how the paused frame looks. That single change in method tells you more than any spec sheet, and it is free.

A note on the lifetime-licence catch

Both one-time options here share an honest downside: updates eventually stop, or a major version becomes a new purchase. That is real and worth saying. The difference from a subscription is what happens at the end — when updates stop on software that runs locally, the copy you installed keeps working on your footage indefinitely, which is not true of anything that phones home to authorise itself or does the processing on someone else's machine.
