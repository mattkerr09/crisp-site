A cheaper VideoProc alternative for Mac

VideoProc Converter AI sells a lifetime licence at $149.90 for one Mac. Crisp is $19 once. Both run locally, both upscale with AI, and both convert formats — so the real question is what the extra $130 buys you, and whether you need it.

What VideoProc is actually for

VideoProc started as a transcoder and DVD ripper, and that heritage still shows. It handles a huge matrix of input and output formats, rips discs, downloads from web sources, and does hardware-accelerated batch conversion very well. The AI upscaling and frame interpolation were bolted onto that foundation later.

If your work is high-volume format wrangling — converting a shelf of old camcorder files, ripping a DVD collection, batch-transcoding a hundred clips overnight — VideoProc is built for exactly that and Crisp is not. Buy VideoProc.

What Crisp is actually for

Crisp is an editor with AI upscaling attached, not a converter with an editor attached. You import clips, cut them on a timeline, build montages and split-screens, colour them, and upscale the finished edit — in one app, on-device.

The difference shows on a normal job. Taking phone footage, trimming it, stacking two angles, and pushing the result to a sharp 4K is a single pass in Crisp. In VideoProc that's a conversion tool doing part of the work and something else doing the rest.

The honest verdict

These tools barely overlap once you look past the shared phrase "AI video enhancer." VideoProc is a conversion powerhouse with AI features. Crisp is an offline editor with AI upscaling. Someone digitising a family archive of tapes and discs wants VideoProc. Someone making videos wants Crisp.

Where Crisp wins outright is the entry cost for the common case. Most people who search for a video upscaler want one thing: make this footage look better without uploading it or subscribing to anything. That's $19 in Crisp, and you can test it on your own clip for free first — the free tier is the whole editor with a small watermark on exports.

The one-Mac licence detail people miss

VideoProc's $149.90 lifetime tier covers a single machine. If you work across a laptop and a desktop, you're either buying the multi-device tier or moving a licence around every time you switch. Crisp's $19 licence activates up to five Macs, which for most people means it simply covers everything they own.

Neither approach is wrong — a one-machine licence is a normal way to price software — but it changes the real comparison. A two-Mac household is not comparing $149.90 to $19.

Speed, and what it costs you

Both apps use Apple Silicon acceleration, so a standard upscale on either is a reasonable wait rather than an overnight job. Crisp's heaviest lane, Max, is the exception: it's a generative restore that's genuinely slow, and it's meant for a short precious clip you're rescuing, not a batch. Use the standard Enhance lane for everyday work and the speed difference between the two apps mostly disappears.

If you're comparing both against Topaz

Worth knowing why this comparison exists at all. Topaz ended perpetual licences in October 2025 and moved to $299/year, which sent a lot of people looking for one-time options. VideoProc at $149.90 and Crisp at $19 are both answers to that, at very different price points and for genuinely different jobs.
