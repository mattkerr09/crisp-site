Where UniFab is the better buy

Discs. UniFab rips DVD and Blu-ray, handles the container and codec mess that comes with them, and Crisp does none of that. If you are digitising a shelf of Blu-rays, this comparison is over — buy UniFab. The same goes if you genuinely use a broad conversion toolkit, because at $319 once it undercuts a year of Topaz Video AI and you get far more surface area.

It is also worth saying plainly that a lifetime licence at any price is a better structure than a subscription. UniFab and Crisp agree on that, and both are on the right side of it.

Where the bundle works against you

Most people who search for a UniFab alternative are not looking for 22 tools. They upscaled some old footage, liked it, and then saw the price. If upscaling and cleanup is the job, $319 buys you a lot of software you will open once out of curiosity.

The Mac question matters more. UniFab's centre of gravity is Windows, where GPU acceleration is built around NVIDIA. On a Mac you want a tool built for Metal and unified memory from the start, not a port. Crisp runs its heaviest mode in about 11GB of peak memory on Apple Silicon, which is what lets it work on a 16GB MacBook Air rather than needing a desktop.

The editing difference

UniFab enhances files. It takes a video in and gives a better video back, and then you open something else to actually cut it. Crisp has a timeline in the same app: trim, split, reorder, drop in stills, apply a look per clip — and the upscale runs once at export against the finished cut.

That ordering is the practical win. Upscaling ten clips and then discovering you only used four of them is wasted GPU time, and on long footage that is not a small waste.

Which to buy

  • Buy UniFab if you rip discs, or if you will genuinely use the wide toolkit, or if you are primarily on Windows.
  • Buy Crisp if you are on a Mac, the job is making footage look better, and you would rather also cut it in the same place. $19, and the free tier is fully functional with a watermark so you can check it works on your own footage before paying.

The thing to check before you buy either

Run your own worst clip through the free tier of both. Not the demo footage on the product page — that footage is chosen because it upscales well. The clips that separate these tools are the ones with fast motion, low light, and a face, because that combination is where a weak model produces either a plastic smoothed face or a shimmering background, and both are obvious once you know to look.

One more practical note on UniFab specifically: check what the licence covers before paying. The all-in-one bundle and the individual modules are sold separately, and it is easy to buy a module expecting the bundle. Crisp has exactly one purchase and it unlocks everything, which is less a feature than an absence of a decision.
